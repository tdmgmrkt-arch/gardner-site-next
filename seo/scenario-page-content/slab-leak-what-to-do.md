# How to Know If You Have a Slab Leak — and What to Do Next

A slab leak is a break in a water line running beneath your home's concrete foundation. The three most common warning signs are a water bill that spiked without explanation, a warm or damp spot on your floor, and the sound of running water when every faucet and fixture in the house is off. If you notice any one of these, don't wait — call a licensed plumber for professional detection before the leak weakens your foundation or drives water into your walls.

## 7 Signs You Have a Slab Leak

<!-- SCHEMA AGENT NOTE: Wrap this section in ItemList JSON-LD. Each sign = one ListItem with name + description. -->

This list covers the most common and most diagnostic symptoms. Each one is a standalone indicator — you don't need all seven to have a problem.

**1. Your water bill spiked with no obvious cause.**
A water bill that jumps $50 – $200 in a single billing cycle, with no change in household usage, points strongly to a pressurized leak under the slab. Hot-water-line slab leaks are especially common in Riverside County because the hard water erodes copper fittings from the inside over time.

**2. You feel a warm or hot spot on your floor.**
Slab leaks on hot-water lines raise the temperature of the concrete directly above the break. Walk barefoot across tile, hardwood, or laminate and note any section that feels noticeably warmer than the rest of the floor. This is one of the most reliable self-diagnostic tests a homeowner can perform.

**3. You hear water running when everything is off.**
Turn off every faucet, appliance, and irrigation zone. Stand in a quiet room. A faint hissing or rushing sound — especially near exterior walls or in the center of the house — suggests water is moving through a pipe it shouldn't be.

**4. Your water pressure dropped suddenly.**
A pressurized line that's breached bleeds pressure before water reaches your fixtures. If your shower suddenly feels weak or faucets run slower than normal, a leak in the supply line — including under the slab — is a likely cause.

**5. You smell mildew in one specific area of the house.**
Water escaping a slab pipe wicks upward through concrete and subfloor. When it reaches organic material — wood framing, carpet pad, drywall — it creates the conditions for mold within 24 – 48 hours. A musty smell concentrated in one room, without a visible water source, is a red flag.

**6. Your flooring is buckling, cracking, or separating.**
Prolonged moisture from a slab leak softens the subfloor and causes tile grout to crack, hardwood planks to buckle, or carpet to develop damp patches. If the damage appears in a pattern — along one wall, in one corner — trace it to the nearest plumbing run.

**7. You notice cracks in walls or a door frame that no longer closes squarely.**
Water under a slab changes the soil's load-bearing capacity. In Riverside County, where expansive clay soil is common in cities like Menifee, Perris, and parts of Corona, even a slow leak can shift a slab enough to crack drywall or rack a door frame. Foundation movement is a late-stage symptom — if you're here, call today.

## What to Do in the Next Hour

<!-- SCHEMA AGENT NOTE: Wrap this section in HowTo JSON-LD. Each step = one HowToStep. -->

**Step 1: Check your water meter with everything off.**
Locate the meter at the street. Turn off every faucet, toilet, appliance, and irrigation zone inside the house. Watch the meter's low-flow indicator (a small triangle or dial) for 60 seconds. If it moves, water is flowing somewhere — likely a leak. Take a photo of the meter reading, wait 30 minutes without using any water, then check again. A rising number confirms a leak.

**Step 2: Note exactly where the warm spot or damp area is.**
Walk the perimeter and interior of the house. Mark the spot with a piece of tape. When a technician arrives with acoustic detection equipment, your observation cuts the diagnostic time from 2 – 3 hours to under an hour. Specific location data matters.

**Step 3: Shut off the water to that zone if you know how.**
If you have individual shut-offs under sinks or at the water heater and the warm spot is near the hot-water side of the house, shutting off the water heater supply can slow the leak. If you're not certain which valve controls which line, shut off the main supply at the street. A few hours without water is far less expensive than a flooded subfloor.

**Step 4: Call for professional leak detection — don't dig, don't drill.**
Slab leaks require acoustic listening equipment, electronic amplification, and sometimes thermal imaging to locate accurately. A homeowner breaking concrete to find a leak they heard "somewhere over there" regularly causes $3,000 – $6,000 in unnecessary repair costs. ## How Gardner Plumbing Co. Detects Slab Leaks Without Jackhammering Your Whole House

Gardner Plumbing Co. uses non-invasive, precision equipment before any concrete is touched. Here's what that means in practice:

- **Acoustic listening equipment** — a trained technician presses a ground microphone against the slab surface and listens for the distinct frequency of pressurized water escaping a pipe. The sound pattern is different from normal water flow and registers differently in copper versus PVC or PEX lines.
- **Electronic amplification** — correlators compare the arrival time of the leak sound at two access points, calculating the exact distance from each end. Combined with pipe-routing maps of your home, this isolates the break to within a foot.
- **Thermal imaging** — on hot-water line breaks, an infrared camera reads the temperature differential in the slab surface. Warm moisture traveling upward creates a visible heat signature. Thermal imaging confirms what acoustic detection locates.
- **Pressure testing** — the suspected line is isolated and pressurized with nitrogen. A pressure drop confirms the leak is on that section, not elsewhere in the system.

The result: Gardner technicians open concrete in a targeted 12 – 18 inch section rather than tearing up a floor to hunt a noise. Less demolition means lower repair cost and a faster return to normal for your household.

## Why Slab Leaks Are So Common in Southern California

Southern California slab foundations create a different set of plumbing risks than basement or crawl-space construction. In Riverside County, two specific factors accelerate the problem.

First, the soil. Much of western Riverside County — particularly Menifee, Perris, and Murrieta — sits on expansive clay soil that swells when wet and contracts when dry. Seasonal moisture cycles cause the slab to shift slightly year after year. That movement stresses the copper joints where supply lines make turns or connect to fittings beneath the concrete. A joint that holds under normal conditions can fail after 15 years of micro-movement.

Second, the water. Riverside County's municipal water supply is consistently hard — high in calcium and magnesium carbonate. Hard water deposits mineral scale on the interior walls of copper pipe, narrowing the diameter over time and accelerating pitting corrosion on the pipe's outer surface where it contacts the concrete. Many homes in Corona, Hemet, and San Jacinto were built in the 1970s through 1990s with copper supply lines that are now 30 – 50 years old and entering their highest-risk window for under-slab failure. A water softener or whole-home filtration system can slow this process — but once the pipe fails, detection and repair are the only path forward.

**Repair:** Varies by method. Three main approaches:

- **Spot repair** ($1,500 – $2,500): The targeted section of pipe is exposed, the damaged section is cut out and replaced, and the concrete is patched. Best for a single isolated break on an otherwise sound pipe.
- **Pipe reroute** ($2,000 – $4,000): A new supply line is run above the slab — through walls or ceiling — bypassing the under-slab pipe entirely. This is often recommended when the pipe is old or has failed in multiple spots, because excavating a compromised 40-year-old copper line tends to reveal additional breaks.
- **Epoxy pipe lining** ($3,000 – $5,000+): A resin liner is pulled through the existing pipe, sealing the interior without excavation. Not appropriate for all pipe sizes or materials — Gardner technicians assess suitability during detection.

**On insurance:** Most standard homeowner's insurance policies (California HO-3 forms) cover the water damage caused by a slab leak — ruined flooring, wet drywall, mold remediation — but do not cover the cost of repairing the pipe itself or accessing it through the concrete. ### Can a slab leak fix itself?

No. A breach in a pressurized pipe does not seal on its own. The leak continues 24 hours a day, every day, until the pipe is repaired. A slow slab leak that shows only a modest water bill increase in month one will typically expand as the surrounding soil softens and the pipe joint shifts further. Early detection and repair is significantly less expensive than addressing the same leak after months of foundation moisture and interior water damage.

### How dangerous is an unrepaired slab leak?

An unrepaired slab leak creates three compounding risks. First, water cost: a moderate slab leak wastes 20 – 50 gallons per day, adding $30 – $90 per month to water bills indefinitely. Second, structure: prolonged moisture under a slab softens the subgrade soil, which in Riverside County's expansive-clay areas can accelerate foundation movement and lead to cracked slabs, sticking doors, and wall cracks. Third, indoor air quality: moisture wicking into subfloors and wall cavities creates conditions for mold growth within 24 – 48 hours of saturation. Mold remediation in a single room costs $1,500 – $5,000 before any plumbing repair is considered.

### How do I prevent slab leaks?

Four measures reduce risk in Riverside County homes. First, install a whole-home water softener — hard water is the primary cause of copper pipe corrosion in this area. Second, maintain water pressure below 80 psi using a pressure-reducing valve; high pressure stresses pipe joints. Third, consider a smart water monitor like Moen Flo, which detects flow anomalies and can shut off the main supply automatically if a leak starts. Fourth, schedule a plumbing inspection on any home over 20 years old with original copper supply lines.
